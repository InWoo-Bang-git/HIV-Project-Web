What does HIV do to your body

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a virus that attacks the immune system, specifically CD4 cells (T cells), which help the immune system fight off infections. HIV can lead to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) if left untreated. Symptoms of HIV can vary widely, and some individuals may not experience any noticeable symptoms for years after contracting the virus.

Stages of HIV

1. Acute HIV Infection (Early Stage):

  • Fever
  • Fatigue
  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Sore throat
  • Muscle and joint aches
  • Headache
  • Diarrhea Nausea and vomiting

These symptoms usually occur within 2 to 4 weeks after exposure to the virus and may resemble flu-like symptoms. Some people may not notice these symptoms or mistake them for another illness.

2. Clinical Latency Stage (Chronic HIV Infection):

  • During this stage, which can last for years, most people with HIV have no specific symptoms.
  • Fatigue
  • The virus continues to replicate at very low levels, and the immune system may gradually weaken.

AIDS (Advanced Stage):

If HIV is left untreated, it can progress to AIDS. AIDS is diagnosed when the immune system is severely damaged, and the person becomes susceptible to opportunistic infections and certain cancers. Symptoms at this stage can include:

  • Rapid weight loss
  • Recurring fever or profuse night sweats
  • Prolonged swelling of lymph nodes
  • Chronic diarrhea
  • Skin problems, including rashes or sores
  • Recurrent respiratory infections
  • Memory loss, depression, and other neurological disorders
